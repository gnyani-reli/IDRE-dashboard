Higher-Payment Cases Are Being Resolved Faster — Not Slower

Logic 1 · Payment Timing Anomaly · Q1 + Q2 2025 · OON Emergency + Air Ambulance
Across 1.2 million qualifying merit decisions, cases where IDREs approved payments furthest above the federal benchmark were resolved in fewer days — not more. Routine cases near the QPA benchmark took a median of 39 days; cases approved at more than 500% of the benchmark took a median of 32 days. This 7-day inversion is present at 10 of 14 certified IDREs, confirming it is systemic rather than isolated. A compliant review process produces the opposite pattern: larger deviations from benchmark should require more scrutiny, not less.

A compliant review process produces an upward slope — larger deviations from benchmark require more scrutiny. The consistent downward trend across all four tiers, confirmed at both the average and median level, indicates high-payment decisions are processed through a lower-scrutiny path regardless of financial magnitude.

IQR spread by tier — P25 → Median → P75
The spread narrows for the Extreme tier (24–48 days) vs. Routine (26–91 days). High-payment cases are not just faster on average — they are consistently fast, inconsistent with individualized case review.

The narrowing interquartile range at the Extreme tier means these decisions are not only faster but more uniform. If IDREs were applying individual case scrutiny, the spread should be wider for high-complexity high-payment cases — not tighter.

Routine cases (near QPA) resolved in median 39 days. Extreme cases (>500% QPA) resolved in median 32 days. Both the average and median show the same inversion — this is not an artifact of outliers. P75 drops from 91 days (Routine) to 48 days (Extreme), meaning even the slowest Extreme cases are resolved faster than the typical Routine case.

Additionally, the Medicare fee schedule cross-check reveals that 81.4% of even Routine-tier cases have a QPA set below 50% of the Medicare rate for the same service — confirming the high payment variance is partly a reflection of an artificially low QPA baseline, not solely provider overasking. The Medicare comparison (Act 3) separates these two explanations at the service code level.

Act 4 — Medicare Benchmark
Which service codes drive high variance — and is the QPA the problem?
For each Extreme-tier service code, the QPA is compared against the Medicare fee schedule. BOTH = QPA below 50% of Medicare AND provider offer above 3× Medicare simultaneously — the insurer is underpaying and the provider is overasking. LOW QPA = high variance explained primarily by artificially low benchmark. The majority of Extreme-tier service codes fall into the BOTH category.
